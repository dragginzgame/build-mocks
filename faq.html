About Toko

What is Toko?

Toko is a digital asset platform on the Internet Computer. It brings together a Generator for building collectibles from layered artwork, a Wallet for holding them, a Launchpad for launching and claiming new collections, and a secondary Marketplace for peer-to-peer trading.

How is Toko structured — Project, Collection, Vendor?

Three layers. A Project is the top-level creator workspace (team, media, whitelists, beneficiaries, and vendors). A Collection is the policy boundary for one set of tokens — its attributes, rarity, and supply. A Vendor is the “stall” that distributes tokens. The Generator sits beside the collection: its job is the art, while the collection owns the rules.

Do I need to sign in?

Public surfaces (Launchpad, collection pages) are browsable, but wallet routes (“My Tokens”) are protected — you sign in with your Internet Computer identity, and ownership always resolves against your principal.
For collectors

Wallet

Where do I see what I own?

In My Tokens, from the authenticated sidebar. It shows the tokens currently owned by your principal, your fungible balances (ICRC1 and NNS), and a transaction history — all in one place.

What can I do with a token I own? WIP

Today: Transfer it to another principal, or Burn it, both with a confirmation step. Selling actions (list for sale / auction) and stack operations (split, unstack, merge, combine, compact) are designed but not finalized.

Is burning reversible?

No. A burn is permanent and the token can't be recovered. The record is kept in ledger history (status Burned) so supply accounting and provenance stay intact.

What are the balances in my wallet?

Your fungible token balances — ICRC1 and NNS — shown with symbol/name and amount. In this version balances are view-only; sending and swapping fungibles is out of scope for now.

What's in the transaction history?

Recent transfers for both your NFTs and fungible balances: type (sent, received, mint, burn), amount, from/to principal, timestamp, and a transaction reference where available. View everything together or filter to NFT-only or fungible-only.

I just claimed a token — when does it appear?

On claim, ownership moves to your principal in the ledger and the token shows up in My Tokens on refresh/reload (or via live update where available).

Why might an action be blocked? WIP

Before any wallet action, Toko re-checks current ownership, status, and lock state. If something changed since the page loaded, the action fails safely with “Token state changed. Refresh and try again.” A token listed for sale is also locked against transfer/burn until you cancel the listing.

Buying

What is the Launchpad?

The public place to discover and claim drops. The default view is a gallery of all live vendor drops, plus a Coming Soon rail and a Recently Ended rail. It's intentionally editorial and collectible-first, not a dense trading screen.

What do the labels mean (Live, Coming soon, Ending soon, Sold out, Ended)?

They're derived from the vendor's real state: Coming soon (scheduled, before start), Live (claimable now), Ending soon (running with roughly ≤2 hours left), Sold out (inventory exhausted), and Ended (shown only during a short retention window after it finishes). Paused and faulted vendors are hidden.

How do I claim a token?

From a vendor's Launchpad detail page. If you're eligible and the drop is live with inventory, the button reads Claim now; the claim is explicit, shows your wallet/payment context before you confirm, and consumes inventory atomically. If you're not eligible it reads View requirements.

Why can't I claim — what are “requirements”?

Creators can attach programmable conditions to a drop: token-holding requirements, staking, an allowlist, or a per-person claim limit. If you don't meet them, the button shows View requirements instead of Claim. Eligibility is checked again live at claim time.

What ranking and discovery sections exist? WIP

Live vendors, Coming Soon, and Recently Ended are available today. Featured, Trending, Newest, and Recently Claimed are planned and the curation tooling isn't built yet.

Trading WIP · subject to change

What is the Marketplace? WIP

Toko's peer-to-peer trading layer for already-minted tokens: collector-to-collector sales, offers, auctions, and price/provenance transparency. It's a projection over ownership and listing state, not a separate ownership system.

Is it custodial — do I hand over my token to list it? WIP

No. It's non-custodial: ownership stays with you until a sale settles. While listed, the token is locked against conflicting actions; cancel the listing to act on it again. Settlement is atomic — it executes fully or fails fully.

What selling formats are planned? WIP

Fixed price (Buy Now), Auction (starting bid, duration, increment, optional buy-now), and Offers (make/accept/refuse/counter). A vendor buyback is modeled as an offer in the same system. If a listing ends unsold, the seller chooses whether it returns to the wallet or burns.

How is this different from the Launchpad? WIP

The Launchpad is how you participate in a drop (primary distribution). The Marketplace is how you buy/trade existing tokens (secondary). The Collection Hub is the context page that summarizes a collection and links out, rather than duplicating either interface.
For creators

Generator

What is the Generator for?

It builds the artwork. You give it ordered layers (background, body, accessories…) each holding parts, set compatibility rules, weighting and caps, then run a batch to assemble unique tokens. When you're happy, you pin the keepers and export them into a collection. The Generator deliberately does not assign rarity or score — those are collection policy.

What does “deterministic / replayable” mean, exactly?

Output is a pure function of configHash, seed, batch type, target count and any CSV input. The same inputs always reproduce the same tokens, and if any input changes the output changes. No duplicate part-combinations occur within a batch, and every export carries a locked snapshot (batch id, seed, config hash, layers/parts, weights, compatibility rules, caps, asset versions) so a result can always be audited or replayed.

How does weighting work, and is weighting the same as rarity?

No — this is the key distinction. Generator weighting only controls how often a part is picked (composition/frequency). Collection rarity is a separate policy about how rare a finished token is. Part weights use presets — Very Infrequent (1), Infrequent (2), Balanced (3, the default), Frequent (5), Very Frequent (8) — or custom numbers. Per-part caps are batch-scoped only, never collection-wide scarcity.

What can the Generator produce in one run, and how do parts combine?

Three batch types: a limited number, a single token, or generate from a CSV (download a template, fill it in, upload and validate). Assembly evaluates each layer's inclusion (always or optional_with_probability), picks parts by weighted choice, then validates compatibility (requires/excludes part or layer) and caps, rejecting invalid candidates until the target count of valid, unique tokens is reached.

How do tokens get from the Generator into a collection?

Only through the Generator's export path — there's no separate import wizard. You preview, pin the keepers, and export the pinned set. Imported tokens arrive in Draft with composition/provenance attached. A collection links to a single generator source; once linked, imports from other generators are rejected. The export hands over composition evidence the attribute system can later map into derived values.

Collections

What are the collection stages, and what changes between them?

Draft → Review → Live. Draft is creator configuration (freely editable). Review is publicly reviewable but still editable. Live is production: core policy locks. A token can only go Live after its collection is Live.

What freezes when a collection goes Live?

At go-live the budget basis and policy lock: Max Supply, rarity type, ratio/accelerator, the unlocked tier set, any definition-count cap, guardian permissions, supply caps, and attributes. Only aesthetic fields stay editable (description, thumbnail, banner) plus issue/copy-number display defaults for future vendors. There's no post-live “expand supply” — set it right before go-live. The roster stays open, though: new definitions can still be authored and set Live against the already-frozen budgets.

What is the Guardian?

A set of collection-scoped, buyer-facing promises: whether the collection allows duplicate tokens, token destruction, transfer restrictions, and sale restrictions. They're guarantees collectors can rely on, locked at Live. (Today these are largely UI configuration + warnings; full backend enforcement of the invariants is still being completed.)

How do supply and issue numbering work?

Supply policy sets a total cap, a max number of unique tokens, and max copies per token. Issue numbers belong to the token definition (not each copy), are assigned in live-order at Review → Live, then locked and never reused. Each copy also gets an immutable copy_sequence. Display options: Hidden (default), Show issue only, or Show issue and supply when capped — non-stackable multi-copy tokens can additionally show a copy number.

Can I delete a collection?

Only if no token has been minted yet (with an explicit confirmation). Once any token is minted, deletion is blocked.

Attributes

What are attributes, token types, and traits?

Attributes are the schema of properties tokens can have, authored at the collection level (not in the Generator). A token type groups tokens that share an attribute set; traits are the specific values. Generator composition is evidence the attribute system maps into derived attribute values via the Generator Mapping surface — the Generator itself never stores attributes on parts.

How do attributes become a token's score?

On the Attributes page you map attribute values to numbers (Apply Values) and choose a calculation method — Add Values, Multiply, or Highest. Missing/unmapped values are excluded (not treated as 0 unless you map them to 0). That produces the attribute score, which feeds the final token score. Whether the score is shown to collectors is a separate per-collection visibility setting.

Rarity

What rarity models are there?

Three, set at the collection level; every collection starts in Uniform. Uniform treats all tokens equally. Tiered lets you define your own tiers (names + colours fully custom) and assign manually — the system shows Final Score and Score Rank as non-binding guidance but offers no “suggested tier”. Weighted uses Toko's fixed official tiers (Common, Uncommon, Rare by default; Epic and Legendary unlockable) and assigns deterministically by score. Switching models requires explicit confirmation and resets rarity outputs.

How is the Weighted distribution decided?

By a ratio (how much scarcer each rarer tier is than the one below) and an accelerator (curve steepness). Per-tier quotas are computed read-only from those over Max Supply — you tune the curve, not raw quotas. Defaults the moment you choose Weighted: Max Supply 1,000, ratio 3, accelerator 1.0, giving roughly Common 69% / Uncommon 23% / Rare 8% on the 3-tier set.

What does a Weighted tier quota actually limit, and when is it enforced?

A quota is a mint budget — a cap on how many copies across the whole tier may ever be minted, not a cap on how many definitions carry that tier. Each definition gets exactly one tier and its own supply. Oversubscription is allowed: you can author more “Rare” definitions than the Rare budget can mint. The budget is drawn down and enforced only at mint, first-come, in one atomic transaction; it's frozen at collection go-live. The Live view shows budget · minted · remaining and a per-definition “mintable now”.

Will my tiers change on their own?

No. There's no silent background re-tiering. On Draft → Review only the transitioning token is auto-assigned; collection-wide redistribution happens only when you run an explicit Rebalance. A manual per-row override pins a token to a tier (and excludes it from rebalance) until you undo it, and is frozen as the token's locked_tier at go-live.

Token score

How is a token's score calculated?

final_score = attribute_score × layer_multiplier. The attribute score comes from the numbers you mapped to attributes, combined by the collection's method (Add Values / Multiply / Highest). The layer multiplier scales by generator-layer weighting authored on the Layer Multipliers tab (it's 1.0 when no generator is linked). Adding more tokens never changes an existing token's score unless that token's own inputs changed.

Is score the same as rank?

No. Score Rank is always computed within a single token type, never across the whole collection — a type with five scoring attributes would otherwise systematically outscore one with a single attribute. Multi-type collections get a token-type filter on the assignment/scoring tables, and the copy spells out the scope (“Score Rank is within Character · 312 tokens”).

What is a “stale score” and why does it block go-live?

Every token is fresh or stale_score. A score goes stale when an input changes — composition or attribute values (while Draft), the value→number mapping, the layer-weight config, or the calculation method. The UI flags it, and a token can't move Review → Live while stale (or while a recompute runs), so live tokens always reflect their current inputs. A bulk Refresh all stale exists; it can partially fail and reports per-token results.

Is the score always visible to collectors?

No — score visibility is a separate per-collection setting, orthogonal to the rarity model.

Vendors

What is a vendor?

A distribution mechanism — the configured “shop” that hands tokens to collectors, with its own storefront, lifecycle, inventory, costs, rules, and revenue. Its inventory is backed by minted tokens, not draft definitions.

Are vendors per-collection or per-project?

Vendors are project-scoped: a single vendor can distribute minted tokens from several of a project's collections. Revenue templates are project-level and claim defaults are collection-level. Each vendor settles and holds buyback proceeds from its own subaccount.

What vendor types are there?

Market vendors (sell specific tokens) launch first. Gacha and GachaRwa (randomized “mystery pull”) are designed but deferred and feature-gated off until a later release — so at launch, expect Market vendors only.

How do Gacha vendors work? WIP

A claim returns a randomly selected reward from the vendor's prize pool. Two draw modes: uniform lucky dip (equal chance per available unit) or weighted (odds from configured_weight × available_quantity, so they shift as inventory changes). The full prize pool — including sold-out entries — is always visible, draws are deterministic and fully audited (replayable from the recorded seed inputs), with optional reveal moments. Not in the first launch.

How do I control who can claim, and how much?

Vendors support claim costs (by tier), requirements (holdings, staking, allowlist — evaluated per tier), restrictions, and rewards, plus per-principal limits. Tier rules inherit from lower tiers where higher entries are missing, and allowlists come from your project whitelists.

What is the vendor lifecycle?

Two axes: a stage (Draft / Review / Live) and a runtime status (setup, running, paused, empty, ended, faulted). You can start, pause, resume, restart, or stop a vendor. There's no separate vendor approval gate — go-live validation happens per token at Review → Live. When a vendor stops, unclaimed inventory either returns to inventory or is burned, per its termination policy.

Revenue

How does revenue work?

Through Revenue Presets — named, project-level split templates authored in Project → Revenue presets. A vendor must select a Live preset at creation, which copies the values onto the vendor as a snapshot; editing the preset later doesn't change vendors already using it. Claim presets ship first; Sale presets are a future tab.

What's in a revenue split?

Fees off the top — Project / cycles funding (required, ~1–10%, funds your project canister) and an optional Toko contribution (0–10%) — plus optional royalties to your beneficiaries. Per-royalty cap ~10%, total royalties ≤ ~20%, and non-project allocations ≤ ~40%. Only Live beneficiaries can receive royalties; to pay yourself, add yourself as a beneficiary. (Percentages are current design values.)

Can royalties change over time?

Yes. Each royalty runs Forever or for a fixed period (1/3/6/9/12/24 months) with a taper — No taper, Cliff, Linear, or Curve — that changes the effective percent across the period. The taper clock starts when the vendor first goes Running.

What is a buyback?

An offer from a vendor to buy a token back, configured at the vendor level (not in the revenue preset) — a vendor percentage in roughly the 1–20% range over a set duration. It runs through the same shared offer system as marketplace offers, and proceeds are held in the vendor subaccount until release.
